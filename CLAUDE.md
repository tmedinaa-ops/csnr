# CSNR Project Direction

## What this project is
Policy analysis at CSNR on SNAP-10A and the regulation of space nuclear systems. Tomas is in the policy lane, not the reactor engineering lane. The through line is how NSPM-20 and its lineage would treat a SNAP-10A class reactor today, and what that means for future nuclear electric propulsion.

## Settled conclusions (do not relitigate without new evidence)
- SNAP-10A's safety mechanism and disposal plan would pass modern regulation. Its operational orbit was its disposal orbit, reached by the Atlas-Agena and verified before startup, and the reactor shut down nuclearly safe in a stable, long-lived orbit. That substantially meets NPR 8715.26, and a high disposal orbit is still an accepted destination.
- The mission ended on a payload fault, the Agena voltage regulator, not the reactor or the disposal, and it failed safe. The one honest caveat is debris, since the later NaK coolant release would draw Orbital Debris Program Office attention.
- HEU and reactor category mean more scrutiny and a higher tier, not a failure.
- The core regulatory shift to make the argument around is the move from a deterministic population dose standard in 1965 to NSPM-20's probabilistic 25 rem at one in a million.

## Current direction: find and test the models they used
Goal is to locate, document, and where feasible reimplement and test the original SNAP-10A and SNAPSHOT analysis models, using the annotated bibliography (NAA-SR-MEMO-12023) as the index.

Models identified so far, with report numbers:
- BOOMER (NAA-SR-8414) reactor kinetics, heat transfer, and hydrogen diffusion, transient response. Confirmed downloadable on OSTI (biblio 4048602). Best starting point.
- TRANCORE-10A (NAA-SR-MEMO-9589) transient core temperatures for startup, scram, loss of flow. Cannot handle near prompt critical.
- FUSAK (NAA-SR-11400) fuel element performance, hydrogen redistribution, dissociation pressure, permeation.
- Shield flux comparison (NAA-SR-MEMO-8768) using DTK transport, AIM-6 diffusion, FMC-N Monte Carlo, and 14-0 removal theory, plus the LiH cross section addendum.
- SNAP 10A Total System Simulation (NAA-SR-MEMO-6721) whole APU model for control specs and stability.
- PSM-2 SNAP 10A Analog Simulator (operations manual) and NAA-SR-9720 prestartup and startup performance, which documents the math models used in both digital and analog solutions.

Approach and constraints:
- The original code is not preserved in any runnable form and was written for 1960s mainframes, so do not plan to run the originals. The realistic path is to pull each report, extract the governing equations, reimplement on the chosen stack, and validate against the documented results in the report.
- Sequence decided June 2026: start with shielding, the only area with both a complete buildable spec and clean validation, then the reactor transient (BOOMER, TRANCORE-10A), then the Total System Simulation for whole-system behavior. Skip the pure data reduction utilities (FSM-1, History Analysis Program, the FORTRAN data decks, FLIGHT-2), they are not physics models.
- Modern reference point for what this looks like with current tools: the 2025 arXiv multiphysics modeling of SNAP-10A with Cardinal and OpenMC (2505.04024).

## Energy conversion modeling and the 14 kWe direction (June 2026)
Energy conversion is now modeled, not just sourced, in CSNR/energy_conversion/.
- Thermoelectric converter built and validated against NAA-SR-11955 Table 2 to under 0.2% (snap10a_te_converter.py, validate_te_converter.py): 581 We, 1.82% overall, 7.65% device, energy balance closes. The back-solved Seebeck was corrected from 518 to ~479 uV/K, since 518 paired the open-circuit voltage with the loaded dT and made circuit power and heat-balance power disagree by 16%. Correction applied to equations 5.2a and base set Section 3; reasoning in Energy_Conversion_Model_Notes.md.
- SiGe temperature-dependent properties are an RTG-grade Si80Ge20 proxy (sige_properties.py, crosscheck_seebeck.py); the real 67/33 As-doped curves stay the classified gap. Seebeck cross-check: proxy ~377 uV/K vs back-solved 479, consistent in magnitude and direction, so the lumped fit is material-physical not a fitting artifact.
- Thermoelectric vs Stirling on the same SNAP reactor is built (stirling_converter.py, compare_te_stirling.py, TE_vs_Stirling_Comparison.md, plus te_vs_stirling_diagram.svg and the comparison and path charts). A Stirling gives 4-6x the electricity (2.3-3.5 vs 0.58 kWe) and ~5-7x specific power, anchored to KRUSTY/Kilopower (23% system at 950/475 K); the radiator barely shrinks. The cost is moving parts, which is why SNAP chose thermoelectric.
- snap10a_system_power.py ties reactor thermal power to kWe and makes the recurring point concrete: OpenMC supplies the heat shape, never the magnitude. SNAP currently makes ~0.58 kWe (34 kWt x 1.82%).

New project constraint: a minimum 14 kWe power requirement. SNAP's ~32 kWt reactor at SNAP temperatures caps at 7.6 kWe by Carnot, so 14 kWe cannot come from a converter swap. The path is Stirling conversion plus a hotter hot side plus a bigger reactor, effectively a higher-power Kilopower, and it ties to the HALEU finding that SNAP's core is leakage-bound so a bigger or hotter core is a redesign regardless. The Stirling concept branch (CSNR/energy_conversion/stirling_cycle_concept/, self-contained like haleu_test) makes reactor temperature and power tweakable knobs, with a temperature-dependent relative efficiency that reproduces both SNAP (7.2%) and Kilopower (23.0%); it shows 14 kWe needs roughly an 80 kWt core at ~1050 K with SNAP's radiator, less if the radiator runs colder.

### Next steps (decided June 2026)
- Methods to increase heat for maximum efficiency. The efficiency lever is hot-side temperature, not heat quantity: a higher T_hot lifts both the Carnot ceiling and the Stirling relative efficiency. Study how to reach the ~1050 K regime, through higher core power density, refractory hot-end materials, and moving off the 775 K pumped NaK loop. Reactor thermal power is the separate output lever, and 14 kWe needs both raised.
- A supplemental sodium loop to carry heat from a hotter reactor. Evaluate a secondary or supplemental sodium heat-transport loop, the KRUSTY sodium-heat-pipe lineage, to deliver heat from a hotter core to the converter hot end; size the flow, the temperature drop, and the pump or wick against the conversion-efficiency gain.
- Simulate the heat transfer in Cardinal/MOOSE. Cardinal now builds and couples natively (Cardinal_MOOSE_Install_Guide.md), so use the MOOSE heat-conduction and fluid energy balance to model the reactor-to-converter thermal path and the sodium loop, with the converter hot-side temperature as the output that feeds the Stirling efficiency model. This is the heat-transport half of the 14 kWe redesign; criticality at the higher power and temperature stays in the snap OpenMC model.
- Constraint to keep attached: the Stirling path trades SNAP's no-moving-parts reliability for a dynamic machine, so carry KRUSTY's many-small-convertors-with-redundancy answer into any 14 kWe design.

## Heat transport model (Cardinal/MOOSE + THM), status June 2026 (Layer 2 built and closed out)
The "simulate the heat transfer in Cardinal/MOOSE" next-step is now built and running in CSNR/heat_transport/. Read README.md there first; it has the architecture, run commands, validation targets, and the layer plan. Built in layers by Tomas's call: a minimum viable model first, then grown, on one architecture so nothing is thrown away.

Architecture: the solid conduction app is the hub; OpenMC and THM are its sub-apps. OpenMC hands the kappa-fission heat to the solid and reads temperature back; THM takes the clad wall temperature and returns the NaK bulk temperature and HTC through the MOOSE CoupledHeatTransfers action with HeatTransferFromExternalAppTemperature1Phase. SI units in MOOSE with scaling=100 to OpenMC's cm. NaK-78 is SimpleFluidProperties from arXiv Table II. The model stops at the NaK outlet on purpose; that temperature is the hot-side input to the energy_conversion TE/Stirling models, since Cardinal does not model the converter.

Layer 0 MVP, validated. mvp/solid.i (2-D RZ single pin) plus mvp/thm.i (one NaK subchannel). Runs in ~9 s with `cardinal-opt -i solid.i` from mvp/. Hits the targets: power_in 918.9 W (= 34 kWt / 37), NaK outlet 817.7 K (62 K rise), max_fuel_T 829 K. The conjugate coupling is energy-consistent: both apps agree on the wall temperature to five figures and the NaK rise closes against the heat source. Validation targets and an independent hand calc are in verify_energy_balance.py.

Heat from the real OpenMC model is wired in. extract_heat_source.py loads the snap model.xml (fig12_test, 783 K), injects an axial kappa-fission mesh tally, runs in a scratch subdir so the validated model.xml is untouched, and writes axial_power.csv. The real axial peaking is 1.40 (flatter than the cosine's 1.57). Those 30 points are embedded in mvp/solid.i as a PiecewiseLinear, and the MVP reproduces 918.9 W on the real shape.

Layer 1 two-way, built and running, not yet confirmed at steady. two_way/ holds a 3-D single reflected pin (snap_unit_pin.py, a k_inf coupling testbed), make_mesh.i for the mesh, openmc.i, solid_3d.i, thm.i. The coupling mechanics are proven: on the last run power_in held at 918.92 W across every step, so OpenMC's heat conserves through the transfer into MOOSE and the THM exchange runs. Convergence was chased through two bugs on June 16 and is fixed in the files but not yet confirmed at steady. First fix: solid_3d.i now uses dt=25, num_steps=60 (1500 s ~= 4.6 thermal time constants) with steady_state_detection REMOVED in favor of fixed steps, because with the stochastic OpenMC source steady_state_detection tripped on Monte Carlo noise (a prior run stopped at t=4 s with the fuel still at its 783 K initial condition). That was necessary but not sufficient: runs kept coming out at dt=1 regardless of the file or the command line. The binding cause, found with a self-logging helper (two_way/run_layer1.sh, which proved the on-disk file had dt=25 yet the master still stepped dt=1), is that the OpenMC sub-app in solid_3d.i is NOT sub_cycling and openmc.i set no dt, so MOOSE clamped the master time step down to the sub-app's default dt=1.0. Fix: openmc.i Executioner now sets dt=1e6 (OpenMC has no time physics here, one k-eigenvalue solve per master step, so a large dt just stops it constraining the master clock). I was wrong twice first blaming a command-line override; the self-logging script is what exposed the sub-app clamp. Outputs were also added: solid_3d.i writes solid_3d.csv and thm.i writes thm_nak.csv, so the plateau and NaK outlet read out without parsing Exodus. Conductivities upgraded the same day and sourced in k_of_T_sources.md: clad is now the ORNL/MSDR Hastelloy N k(T) correlation (9.77 - 3.2e-4 T + 1.46e-5 T^2, reproduces arXiv Table II's 18.852 at ~800 K, replaces the wrong 23.6 that was that curve read at 700 C); fuel 22.484 and coating 1.729 now match arXiv Table II and stay constant because the literature gives no defensible T-curve (U-ZrH is ~flat per Simnad). NEXT ACTION: from two_way/ in the moose env with OPENMC_CROSS_SECTIONS set, run `bash run_layer1.sh` (no Executioner overrides; it clears stale CSVs and tees run_layer1.log) and confirm the plateau: the time column stepping 25/50/.../1500, power_in 918.9 W, NaK outlet ~817 K, max_fuel_T ~826-832 K (flat axial power puts the hot spot near the outlet, unlike the peaked MVP). The MVP still carries the old 18/1.5/23.6 constants, and the openmc.i dt=1e6 fix is two_way-only.

Bugs found and fixed today, so they do not recur. The snap model is a single combined model.xml, not separate geometry/materials/settings files, so the extractor loads it with from_model_xml and runs in a scratch dir rather than looking for materials.xml. THM names its velocity vel_x/vel_y/vel_z, there is no scalar `vel`, so Exodus output lists are 'T T_wall Hw'. The ConcentricCircleMeshGenerator already provides the clad outer surface as the sideset 'outer' (960 faces); a radius-based ParsedGenerateSideset over-selected internal clad faces (2880), so solid_3d.i couples on 'outer'. An OpenMC tally trigger needs max_batches, so the trigger was removed and the run uses the fixed batch count.

Environments: Cardinal runs in the moose conda env (NOT openmc-env); the Python model build and extraction use openmc-env; cardinal-opt is at ~/cardinal/cardinal-opt.

Layer 2 status June 2026, built and closed out analytically. The full 37-pin coupled model is in heat_transport/layer2_core/; openmc.i is the entry point now, not solid_core.i. It went through four rounds documented in layer2_core/Layer2_Fix_Notes.md, and the lessons matter for the uprate work that follows. The architecture was restructured from solid-as-main to OpenMC-as-main with the solid and THM as sub-apps (the Cardinal gas_assembly pattern), because solid-main was a fully-lagged Jacobi sweep that ran ~3 hours and would not converge; OpenMC-main cut the eigenvalue solves from ~350 to ~15. The flat-radial symptom was NOT the neutronics: OpenMC's per-pin power is correctly peaked ~1.56x (confirmed in ParaView) and cell_level=1 is correct. The cause was the THM coupling: the CoupledHeatTransfers action cannot partition one shared 'outer' sideset across 37 channels (an untested MOOSE mode that collapses to a whole-boundary average, verified against the MOOSE source), so solid_core.i is now GENERATED by gen_per_pin.py into 37 per-pin sidesets plus 37 single-channel couplings plus 37 single-instance THM apps, the only source-tested configuration (mesh carve verified, 37x960 faces). The remaining wall is the conjugate convergence: with htc 5e4 the coupling is brutally stiff (the solid sheds 34 kW across a ~0.6 K wall-fluid gap, so the wall T must converge to ~0.01 K), and the coupled fluid landed at 12 to 21 kW against the solid's 34, never converging in a 2-hour run. Because the neutronics and per-pin power are validated, the result was closed out analytically with heat_transport/hot_channel_analytic.py (per-channel energy balance plus 1-D radial conduction), which is what the coupled solve reproduces at convergence: at 34 kWt the converter hot side (mixed-mean NaK outlet) is 817.7 K, the hot-channel outlet 852.6 K, the peak fuel 867 K, all ~100 K under the ~970 K hydride wall, and the peak fuel stays under the wall even at 46 kWt (the first quantitative hint of uprate headroom). Full record in heat_transport/Layer2_Report.md (inputs, code chunks, figures); the 14 kWe synthesis with verified Stirling numbers, the two routes, and the cold-side lever is in energy_conversion/Path_to_14kWe_Briefing.md.

Next steps for the model, in priority order, where Layer 2 feeds the 14 kWe effort. (1) Highest value is Phase 1 of the uprate roadmap (energy_conversion/Power_Uprate_Verification_Roadmap.md), the thermal-hydraulic uprate ceiling, because it collapses most of the 14 kWe band and decides between the two routes: a same-hardware uprate to ~85 kWt at 800 K giving ~13 kWe, versus a hotter ~56 kWt / 1050 K redesign giving ~14 kWe. It needs three pieces the Cardinal model does not yet have, all to be built: temperature-dependent NaK-78 properties (currently room-T constants, a known gap), a tight-lattice (P/D 1.008) pressure-drop and friction model so flow costs pumping head, and the EM pump head-flow curve from NAA-SR-11879 (OSTI 4516323, not yet retrieved). Then sweep the OpenMC kappa-fission magnitude upward holding inlet at 755 K and read three limits at each power: peak fuel vs the ~970 K hydride wall, peak clad vs the Hastelloy-N limit, and required NaK flow vs what the pump delivers into the rising pressure drop. Output: the maximum sustainable kWt at 800 K, turning the assumed 2.5x uprate into a computed number. (2) Finish the coupled conjugate convergence so the fully-coupled fields exist as validation, not only the analytic close-out: switch the solid from FullSolveMultiApp to a warm-started TransientMultiApp so the stiff conjugate accumulates across outer steps instead of resetting cold, and add interface-temperature relaxation (transformed_variables='T_fluid'); the warm-start is how the single-pin Layer 1 converged this same coupling, so tune it cheaply on the single pin before a full-core run. (3) Power flattening (Phase 3): the 1.56x radial peak caps the reactor, so study fuel zoning or hot-channel orificing to raise the average power at the fixed hot-pin limit, a multiplier that stacks on the uprate, re-run on the coupled model. (4) Chain to the Stirling (Phase 5): feed the Phase 1 maximum kWt and its hot-side temperature into the energy_conversion Stirling model at the chosen radiator temperature for the verified kWe, replacing the assumed number, and size the radiator and mass at that cold side. Reactivity and control (drum worth, excess reactivity and the temperature coefficient, the HALEU U_MULT makeup sweep) run in parallel in snap.py per Phase 2. The shared prerequisite across the uprate runs and trusting the coupled hot-side absolutes is the temperature-dependent NaK-78 properties, so build those first.
