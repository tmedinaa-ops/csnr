# CSNR Project Direction

## What this project is
Policy analysis at CSNR on SNAP-10A and the regulation of space nuclear systems. Tomas is in the policy lane, not the reactor engineering lane. The through line is how NSPM-20 and its lineage would treat a SNAP-10A class reactor today, and what that means for future nuclear electric propulsion.

## Settled conclusions (do not relitigate without new evidence)
- SNAP-10A's safety mechanism and disposal plan would pass modern regulation. Its operational orbit was its disposal orbit, reached by the Atlas-Agena and verified before startup, and the reactor shut down nuclearly safe in a stable, long-lived orbit. That substantially meets NPR 8715.26, and a high disposal orbit is still an accepted destination.
- The mission ended on a payload fault, the Agena voltage regulator, not the reactor or the disposal, and it failed safe. The one honest caveat is debris, since the later NaK coolant release would draw Orbital Debris Program Office attention.
- HEU and reactor category mean more scrutiny and a higher tier, not a failure.
- The core regulatory shift to make the argument around is the move from a deterministic population dose standard in 1965 to NSPM-20's probabilistic 25 rem at one in a million.

## Current direction: find and test the models they used
Goal is to locate, document, and where feasible reimplement and test the original SNAP-10A and SNAPSHOT analysis models, using the annotated bibliography (NAA-SR-MEMO-12023) as the index.

Models identified so far, with report numbers:
- BOOMER (NAA-SR-8414) reactor kinetics, heat transfer, and hydrogen diffusion, transient response. Confirmed downloadable on OSTI (biblio 4048602). Best starting point.
- TRANCORE-10A (NAA-SR-MEMO-9589) transient core temperatures for startup, scram, loss of flow. Cannot handle near prompt critical.
- FUSAK (NAA-SR-11400) fuel element performance, hydrogen redistribution, dissociation pressure, permeation.
- Shield flux comparison (NAA-SR-MEMO-8768) using DTK transport, AIM-6 diffusion, FMC-N Monte Carlo, and 14-0 removal theory, plus the LiH cross section addendum.
- SNAP 10A Total System Simulation (NAA-SR-MEMO-6721) whole APU model for control specs and stability.
- PSM-2 SNAP 10A Analog Simulator (operations manual) and NAA-SR-9720 prestartup and startup performance, which documents the math models used in both digital and analog solutions.

Approach and constraints:
- The original code is not preserved in any runnable form and was written for 1960s mainframes, so do not plan to run the originals. The realistic path is to pull each report, extract the governing equations, reimplement on the chosen stack, and validate against the documented results in the report.
- Sequence decided June 2026: start with shielding, the only area with both a complete buildable spec and clean validation, then the reactor transient (BOOMER, TRANCORE-10A), then the Total System Simulation for whole-system behavior. Skip the pure data reduction utilities (FSM-1, History Analysis Program, the FORTRAN data decks, FLIGHT-2), they are not physics models.
- Modern reference point for what this looks like with current tools: the 2025 arXiv multiphysics modeling of SNAP-10A with Cardinal and OpenMC (2505.04024).

## Energy conversion modeling and the 14 kWe direction (June 2026)
Energy conversion is now modeled, not just sourced, in CSNR/energy_conversion/.
- Thermoelectric converter built and validated against NAA-SR-11955 Table 2 to under 0.2% (snap10a_te_converter.py, validate_te_converter.py): 581 We, 1.82% overall, 7.65% device, energy balance closes. The back-solved Seebeck was corrected from 518 to ~479 uV/K, since 518 paired the open-circuit voltage with the loaded dT and made circuit power and heat-balance power disagree by 16%. - SiGe temperature-dependent properties are an RTG-grade Si80Ge20 proxy (sige_properties.py, crosscheck_seebeck.py); the real 67/33 As-doped curves stay the classified gap. Seebeck cross-check: proxy ~377 uV/K vs back-solved 479, consistent in magnitude and direction, so the lumped fit is material-physical not a fitting artifact.
- Thermoelectric vs Stirling on the same SNAP reactor is built (stirling_converter.py, compare_te_stirling.py, TE_vs_Stirling_Comparison.md, plus te_vs_stirling_diagram.svg and the comparison and path charts). A Stirling gives 4-6x the electricity (2.3-3.5 vs 0.58 kWe) and ~5-7x specific power, anchored to KRUSTY/Kilopower (23% system at 950/475 K); the radiator barely shrinks. The cost is moving parts, which is why SNAP chose thermoelectric.
- snap10a_system_power.py ties reactor thermal power to kWe and makes the recurring point concrete: OpenMC supplies the heat shape, never the magnitude. SNAP currently makes ~0.58 kWe (34 kWt x 1.82%).

New project constraint: a minimum 14 kWe power requirement. SNAP's ~32 kWt reactor at SNAP temperatures caps at 7.6 kWe by Carnot, so 14 kWe cannot come from a converter swap. The path is Stirling conversion plus a hotter hot side plus a bigger reactor, effectively a higher-power Kilopower, and it ties to the HALEU finding that SNAP's core is leakage-bound so a bigger or hotter core is a redesign regardless. The Stirling concept branch (CSNR/energy_conversion/stirling_cycle_concept/, self-contained like haleu_test) makes reactor temperature and power tweakable knobs, with a temperature-dependent relative efficiency that reproduces both SNAP (7.2%) and Kilopower (23.0%); it shows 14 kWe needs roughly an 80 kWt core at ~1050 K with SNAP's radiator, less if the radiator runs colder. ## Heat transport model (Cardinal/MOOSE + THM), status June 2026 (Layer 2 built and closed out)
The "simulate the heat transfer in Cardinal/MOOSE" next-step is now built and running in CSNR/heat_transport/. Read README.md there first; it has the architecture, run commands, validation targets, and the layer plan. Built in layers by Tomas's call: a minimum viable model first, then grown, on one architecture so nothing is thrown away.

Architecture: the solid conduction app is the hub; OpenMC and THM are its sub-apps. OpenMC hands the kappa-fission heat to the solid and reads temperature back; THM takes the clad wall temperature and returns the NaK bulk temperature and HTC through the MOOSE CoupledHeatTransfers action with HeatTransferFromExternalAppTemperature1Phase. SI units in MOOSE with scaling=100 to OpenMC's cm. NaK-78 is SimpleFluidProperties from arXiv Table II. The model stops at the NaK outlet on purpose; that temperature is the hot-side input to the energy_conversion TE/Stirling models, since Cardinal does not model the converter.

Layer 0 MVP, validated. mvp/solid.i (2-D RZ single pin) plus mvp/thm.i (one NaK subchannel). Runs in ~9 s with `cardinal-opt -i solid.i` from mvp/. Hits the targets: power_in 918.9 W (= 34 kWt / 37), NaK outlet 817.7 K (62 K rise), max_fuel_T 829 K. The conjugate coupling is energy-consistent: both apps agree on the wall temperature to five figures and the NaK rise closes against the heat source. Validation targets and an independent hand calc are in verify_energy_balance.py.

Layer 2 status June 2026, built and closed out analytically. The full 37-pin coupled model is in heat_transport/layer2_core/; openmc.i is the entry point now, not solid_core.i. It went through four rounds documented in layer2_core/Layer2_Fix_Notes.md, and the lessons matter for the uprate work that follows. The architecture was restructured from solid-as-main to OpenMC-as-main with the solid and THM as sub-apps (the Cardinal gas_assembly pattern), because solid-main was a fully-lagged Jacobi sweep that ran ~3 hours and would not converge; OpenMC-main cut the eigenvalue solves from ~350 to ~15. The flat-radial symptom was NOT the neutronics: OpenMC's per-pin power is correctly peaked ~1.56x (confirmed in ParaView) and cell_level=1 is correct. The cause was the THM coupling: the CoupledHeatTransfers action cannot partition one shared 'outer' sideset across 37 channels (an untested MOOSE mode that collapses to a whole-boundary average, verified against the MOOSE source), so solid_core.i is now GENERATED by gen_per_pin.py into 37 per-pin sidesets plus 37 single-channel couplings plus 37 single-instance THM apps, the only source-tested configuration (mesh carve verified, 37x960 faces). The remaining wall is the conjugate convergence: with htc 5e4 the coupling is brutally stiff (the solid sheds 34 kW across a ~0.6 K wall-fluid gap, so the wall T must converge to ~0.01 K), and the coupled fluid landed at 12 to 21 kW against the solid's 34, never converging in a 2-hour run. Because the neutronics and per-pin power are validated, the result was closed out analytically with heat_transport/hot_channel_analytic.py (per-channel energy balance plus 1-D radial conduction), which is what the coupled solve reproduces at convergence: at 34 kWt the converter hot side (mixed-mean NaK outlet) is 817.7 K, the hot-channel outlet 852.6 K, the peak fuel 867 K, all ~100 K under the ~970 K hydride wall, and the peak fuel stays under the wall even at 46 kWt (the first quantitative hint of uprate headroom). Full record in heat_transport/Layer2_Report.md (inputs, code chunks, figures); the 14 kWe synthesis with verified Stirling numbers, the two routes, and the cold-side lever is in energy_conversion/Path_to_14kWe_Briefing.md.

(2) Finish the coupled conjugate convergence so the fully-coupled fields exist as validation, not only the analytic close-out: switch the solid from FullSolveMultiApp to a warm-started TransientMultiApp so the stiff conjugate accumulates across outer steps instead of resetting cold, and add interface-temperature relaxation (transformed_variables='T_fluid'); the warm-start is how the single-pin Layer 1 converged this same coupling, so tune it cheaply on the single pin before a full-core run. ## System loop: reactor to radiator in one MOOSE solve (June 2026)
The "chain to the Stirling" next-step (Phase 5 above) is now built as a transient THM system loop in heat_transport/system_loop/ (read README.md there first). It carries the heat past the NaK outlet that the rest of heat_transport stops at: reactor fission source -> primary NaK -> Stirling hot end -> rejection NaK -> radiator -> 4 K space, single NaK loop (no IHX), cold side closed to space. The Stirling is a heat-extraction component, not a solved gas cycle (MOOSE solves conduction and 1-D flow, not the closed cycle); the electricity/waste split is the project's validated eta = carnot*rel law, recomputed every timestep from the live NaK temperatures by the THM ControlLogic block (CopyPostprocessorValueControl + SetComponentRealValueControl driving a controllable TotalPower cold-end source). Reactor, engine hot end, and cooler are each a TotalPower -> HeatSourceFromTotalPower -> HeatStructureCylindrical coupled to a FlowChannel1Phase by HeatTransferFromHeatStructure1Phase (positive power heats, negative is a sink); the radiator is HSBoundaryRadiation to 4 K with the 26 m2 flat-panel area carried by the scale factor. Recirculating companion BUILT June 2026 (system_loop_closed.i): the NaK loops back, each loop's return temperature fed into its own inlet via SetComponentRealValueControl on the controllable InletMassFlowRateTemperature1Phase T (verified controllable in docs), flow imposed at the EM-pump rate (the robust "thermal recirculation" choice; a full momentum ring with Pump1Phase + ring junctions is the higher-fidelity alternative). ## Pump replacement and net-energy analysis (June 26 2026)
This thread extended the uprate workstream from "can the existing core make more heat" to "can we get the heat out and turn it into more electricity, and what pump does that take." It sized the pump duty, found a real replacement for the SNAP thermoelectric pump, separated the pumping and plumbing levers, and computed the net electrical gain after the pump pays its own way. All of it is analytic and runs on the Mac in heat_transport/uprate/. New code: plot_flow_temp_power.py, plot_pump_comparison.py, energy_per_pump.py, plus an ALIP model added to em_pump_curve.py.

How fast the uprate pumps NaK. Design flow is 0.6199 kg/s (~13 gpm). At the EOL uprate point (79 kWt, ~897 K outlet) the thermoelectric EM pump delivers ~0.64 kg/s, BOL coupling reaches ~0.70 to 0.74 kg/s (~16 gpm). The pump cannot be pushed harder on command: it is thermoelectric, so its head-flow curve only lifts with NaK temperature, and the hot side is already fuel-limited. With the given pump and the given loop the practical ceiling is ~0.7 kg/s BOL, ~0.6 kg/s EOL, only ~1.3x the design flow. The only knob that raises its flow is running the core hotter, which is the same lever the 970 K fuel limit caps, so the pump ceiling and the fuel ceiling are the same constraint.

NaK is not the wear problem, temperature is. NaK velocity at these flows is ~0.5 to 0.56 m/s, an order of magnitude below the liquid-metal erosion-corrosion threshold (Na/NaK design ceilings ~6 to 8 m/s, negligible below ~3). "Abrasiveness" is the wrong model for a single-phase liquid metal. The real degradation is mass-transport corrosion (dissolution and redeposition of Ni and Cr from the Hastelloy-N driven by the temperature gradient), which is Arrhenius in temperature and only weakly flow-dependent (~Re^0.8). So the uprate's corrosion cost comes from running hotter, not from pumping faster. The build has no NaK corrosion or mass-transfer model; it is a defensible gap given the low velocity and well-characterized Hastelloy-N/NaK behavior, to be built on temperature and oxygen content if a reviewer pushes on materials compatibility.

The loop resistance is not the core. Charging the loop with K from the SNAP design point (7.58 kPa at 13 gpm), the bare core friction is only ~0.6 kPa, ~7.9% of the loop K; the other ~92% is piping, the converter manifold, bends, and form losses. So "larger-bore core plumbing" does nothing (the lattice is also maxed at P/D 1.008 and cannot widen). The plumbing lever has to target the 92% outside the core.

Replacement pump: the answer is the FSP ALIP. The pump that fits is NASA/INL's annular linear induction pump for the Affordable Fission Surface Power reactor, which is explicitly SNAP-10A-derived and runs the same NaK-78 (NTRS 20100035100; design 4.3 kg/s at 68 kPa on the FSP loop, tested 2.5 to 90 gpm, <1 to 90 kPa, NaK to 525 to 650 C, ~6% efficiency, no moving parts). Its measured head-flow curve is now in em_pump_curve.py from Geng & Reid 2017 (NTRS 20170000670, Table I, 100 V/18 A/36 Hz: stall 67.7 kPa, slope 0.775 kPa/gpm, free-delivery 87.5 gpm), with new functions alip_head() and alip_operating_flow(K, n_series, head_factor, solid_torpedo) and a self-check that reproduces the three Table I points. On SNAP's tight loop the ALIP is head-limited to ~1.5 kg/s (2.4x design), because SNAP's loop is ~7x more resistive than the FSP loop every candidate was built for (SNAP demands ~162 kPa at 60 gpm where the FSP loop needed ~22). Candidates ruled second: the FSP mechanical centrifugal NaK pump (NTRS 20110015702; 4 kg/s, 4 m head, ~30% efficient, but SmCo magnets cap ~350 C with active cooling, plus bearings and seals), the SP-100 thermoelectric EM pump (self-powered but cannot scale flow or head), and CMI Novacast (commercial but terrestrial). Recommendation: a single off-the-shelf ALIP.

Pumping vs plumbing are comparable and they multiply. Two levers move the flow wall: a stronger pump (TEM 0.70 to ALIP 1.50 to two ALIPs in series 1.94 kg/s) and a lower loop K (re-plumb to K/4 gives 2.43 kg/s, up to the ALIP free-delivery 4.2 kg/s as K goes to zero). Neither dominates; the productive move is both. Re-plumbing must re-size the 92% (piping bores, converter manifold, bends), which is a loop and converter redesign carrying NaK-inventory and mass penalties, not a bolt-on.

Net electricity after parasitic draw (energy_per_pump.py, radiator 475 K, hot end = NaK outlet - 25 K, fuel limit 970 K). Each pump unlocks a fuel-limited cooling ceiling, but pushing flow cools the hot side (895 to 811 K, Stirling overall 19.6% to 15.7%) and the ALIP charges ~1.5 kWe parasitic at 6% efficiency:
- Current TEM: 0.70 kg/s, 85 kWt -> 15.6 kWe net (baseline, self-powered, no electrical parasitic)
- FSP mechanical: 1.05 kg/s, 120 kWt -> 21.4 kWe (+5.9)
- FSP ALIP (measured): 1.50 kg/s, 148 kWt -> 24.3 kWe (+8.7)
- ALIP x2 series: 1.94 kg/s, 166 kWt -> 24.6 kWe (+9.0)
- ALIP + re-plumb K/4: 2.43 kg/s, 181 kWt -> 27.5 kWe (+11.9), at half the parasitic of the series
- ALIP free-delivery (K->0, idealized): 4.2 kg/s, 208 kWt -> 30.7 kWe
Headline: thermal unlocked rises ~2.4x but net electrical only ~1.8x, because efficiency falls and the pump skims the top. Position: a single ALIP is the best off-the-shelf swap; re-plumbing beats staging decisively (more kWe at half the parasitic). One claim retracted: the earlier 90 kPa/90 gpm envelope estimate had "two ALIPs in series net less than one"; with the measured curve that flips to break-even, so staging is a wash, not a loss, while re-plumb stays the clear winner.

The binding caveat. These are cooling-and-conversion-side ceilings. They assume the reactor can actually produce 120 to 209 kWt, which the leakage-bound core almost certainly cannot without more fissile loading or a redesign (per the HALEU finding). Treat the net-kWe figures as what the power system delivers IF the reactor makes the heat, an upper bound the reactor side has to earn and this analysis does not address. The radiator also balloons (rejecting 100 to 175 kWt at 475 K), so specific power falls even as kWe rises.

Thermoelectric pump question. A self-powered thermoelectric pump moves the pump cost off the electrical ledger and onto the thermal ledger; it reduces the penalty but does not negate it, because the hydraulic work W = Q*dP still has to be supplied. The crossover is eta_TE > eta_Stirling x eta_ALIP ~ 1.1%; at SNAP-like 2% TE efficiency the effective penalty roughly halves (~1.4 to ~0.8 kWe at the single-ALIP point), at ~1% it is a wash. The practical objection dominates: a TEM cannot reach the uprate duty (~2.4x the flow at ~5.7x the head, ~14x SNAP's TEM hydraulic power, against intrinsically low TEM head) and degrades ~13%/yr. The lever that lowers the pump bill in any currency is cutting W itself, which is the re-plumb lever again.

Files (heat_transport/uprate/): plot_flow_temp_power.py + flow_temp_power_map.png (coolant operating map, flow vs outlet T vs heat displaced, with the TEM pump wall and fuel limit), plot_pump_comparison.py + pump_comparison_overlay.png + pump_comparison_panels.png (the same map per replacement pump, with the five flow walls and the plumbing lever), energy_per_pump.py + energy_per_pump.png (net kWe per pump after parasitic). ALIP model is in em_pump_curve.py. Open items: reactor-side feasibility of the higher kWt is the binding unknown; the ALIP curve is the 100 V point, so 120 V drive or the solid-torpedo +16% head recovers ~1.6 to 1.7 kg/s; the CFD that produced the ALIP curve is optimistic at low flow, exactly where SNAP's tight loop puts the pump, so 1.5 kg/s is a mild over-estimate; no NaK corrosion model yet.

Flow-velocity erosion check on the pump comparison (June 29 2026, prompted by an advisor's claim that NaK "acts as an abrasive at a certain pump rate"). Resolved and quantified. The claim is a loose recollection of velocity-dependent erosion-corrosion (flow-assisted corrosion): above a critical velocity wall shear strips the protective film faster than it reforms. Real physics, but "abrasive" is the wrong mechanism (single-phase NaK has no particulates, it is film removal plus mass transport), and the threshold sits an order of magnitude above SNAP. Peak core-channel velocity was computed at each flow wall (the 1.008 P/D lattice is the narrowest, fastest part of the loop, so the worst case): V = (mdot/37)/(rho*A_channel), A_channel 47.65 mm2, rho at loop-mean 785 K, so V is linear in flow. Results: design 0.47, TEM 0.52, FSP mechanical 0.79, single ALIP 1.12, ALIP x2 1.45, re-plumb K/4 1.82, free-delivery 3.15 m/s. Thresholds expressed as the total flow that drives the channel to them: 2 m/s film-strip onset (conservative, borrowed from lead-bismuth, which is far more aggressive than NaK's near-inert film) at 2.66 kg/s = 4.3x design; 6 m/s Na/NaK design ceiling (FIV + erosion-corrosion) at 7.99 kg/s = 12.9x design. Verdict: every realistic pump is safe (TEM through ALIP x2 are 1.4x to 3.8x under even the conservative onset); re-plumb K/4 is the one to flag, it walks the channel to the 2 m/s onset but is still ~3x under the real NaK ceiling, so if that branch is committed check duct and converter-manifold local velocities not just the channel mean; only the unachievable free-delivery case crosses 2 m/s. The high-flow cases are reactor-limited before they are erosion-limited (the leakage-bound core cannot make the 120-208 kWt those flows assume, per the HALEU finding), so fissile loading sets the right edge of the chart, not coolant velocity. This extends and quantifies the existing "NaK is not the wear problem, temperature is" position: the uprate's real materials cost stays temperature-driven (Hastelloy-N mass-transport corrosion is Arrhenius and only ~Re^0.8 in flow, plus hydrogen redistribution 2-3x faster at the hotter fuel), not velocity. Confirmed by pulling both ALIP reports in full: Polzin et al. 2010 (NTRS 20100035100) and Geng & Reid 2017 (NTRS 20170000670) document a SNAP-10A-derived NaK-78 ALIP qualified to 90 gpm at up to 650 C for an 8-year design life with NO flow-velocity erosion or wear limit flagged anywhere, a smooth annular duct with the torpedo encased in a stainless sleeve, no impeller and no cavitating throat (and SNAP's own DC conduction pump likewise has no moving parts), so the classic pump-erosion mechanism does not exist in either pump. plot_pump_comparison.py now carries a peak core-velocity readout on every pump row (overlay legend and panel titles) plus a dashed 2 m/s erosion-onset reference line; full reasoning, table, and citations are in Pump_Erosion_Velocity_Note.md. Caveat on the readout: it is evaluated at a fixed 785 K loop mean, so it is exact as a flow proxy but not the per-operating-point velocity (real density shifts with each wall's outlet temperature); it changes no verdict. The "no NaK corrosion model yet" open item above stands and is the defensible gap if a reviewer pushes on materials compatibility.
